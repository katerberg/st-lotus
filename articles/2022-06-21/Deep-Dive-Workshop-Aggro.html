Deep Dive: Workshop Aggro

Written by: CommonOpponent on June 21, 2022

Fundamental to understanding any meta-game is a familiarity with the capabilities, limitations, and weaknesses of the most aggressive viable strategies. Vintage Rotisserie Draft is no different, so I’d like to give a thorough look at a class of aggressive decks with many common threads, all best described as Mishra’s Workshop-based aggressive decks. Thanks to the VRD card pool and the history of artifact-heavy decks across multiple formats, we can concoct a number of variants on this theme. I’ll be categorizing those variants into three larger buckets.

The tactic with the longest tradition of Vintage domination is Prison, which seeks to create mana disparity by deploying effects like Sphere of Resistance and Phyrexian Revoker in multiples. Next we have Aggro-Control variants, which prefer to leave mana up to counter key spells and protect attackers rather than tapping out for impediments. Finally, Modular Workshop leans into the augmentation and exploitation of numerous low cost modular creatures.

Prison Shops

The name of the game for this classic build is to limit our opponents’ game actions. All the fast mana gobbled up in the early draft is used to deploy preemptive roadblocks to our opponents’ ability to respond or develop meaningful board presence. This deck will typically use as its primary color for premium removal and complementary creatures.

Unlike the other builds, this one is not focused on speed and is probably not capable of turn 3 kills in live action. I think these types of Prison effects are best left to Constructed formats where they benefit from the redundancy of multiple copies. Especially with the arrival of Boseiju, Who Endures, this is the least appealing application of Workshop.

In fact, I believe this type of mid-range Shops deck is entirely obsoleted (for now) by Eldrazi. I’d already argue that these types of decks should be playing Matter Reshaper and Thought-Knot Seer, and most threats dropped off a Workshop aren’t going to beat as hard as Reality Smasher. Just trade in your Steel Overseers and Stonecoil Serpents for Eldrazi Mimics and Endless Ones and save yourself a couple picks on artifact lands. You can even keep the Phyrexian Revoker.

If you do choose this avenue, my only advice would be keep in mind the section about cards later on.

Aggro-Control Shops

Here the goal is to rapidly overwhelm the opponent with a couple of potent threats, then use countermagic to protect them or card advantage spells to refuel and try again once the, then depend on countermagic to protect them, and/or card advantage to refuel and try again once the opponent is out of action.

Any hand with a turn one Patchwork Automaton can kill quickly, and this deck can threaten just that with the added bonus of counterspell support. While this variant can produce plenty of explosive draws, it can also realistically win off a slower start by taking a more reactive stance in the early game. The fast clock of Cranial Plating is often just as effective after an initial exchange as it would be when going in blind against an opponent who might be looking to go over the top with a quick Tinker or Reanimate.

Modular Shops

Modular Shops, perhaps the most resilient build of Aggro Shops, leans into the modular ability and focuses on enhancing its impact.

The biggest drawback here is that we really need things to go our way with the mana in order to get greedy and stretch across four colors for all our pieces. When the mana comes together, though, this variant is an absolute blast that can offer ferocious starts.

In Kamigawa: Neon Dynasty, some cards made reference to or gave bonuses to “augmented” creatures. With +1/+1 counters qualifying as augmentations, fans of Modular Shops should keep an eye out for cards using this terminology in future sets, as any additional cheap disruption or card advantage would be a potential boon to this deck. The Skeleton

Here are the core cards which will be vital to the success of any Workshop-based Aggro deck. The cards assembled around them can cause those decks to function in different ways, but they will all face similar threats and meta-game pressures based on these central tools.

However a player chooses to adorn this frame, I would offer the following guidance based on my examination of the card pool and study of past drafts.

Always Blue

Any variant should contain for best chances to succeed. VRD is a game of cheap threats and cheaper answers, and any deck aiming to beat down needs to be able to defend its threats and refuel after an exchange. Thought Monitor is a rare card with broader applications than current demand on it would indicate, and as of this writing Thoughtcast is still severely under-appreciated. The selection of cheap disruption offers is also vital enough to spend precious early picks in order to secure. The supply of Spell Pierce-adjacent cards is slim enough that it’s entirely reasonable to pick Spell Pierce before almost anything besides your key mana sources.

These types of Workshop decks enjoy an advantage relative to other aggressive decks in the meta in regards to disruption. There are two particular pieces of countermagic which this deck is well positioned to utilize. First is Stubborn Denial which is quite castable as a pseudo-Force Spike but should be live when it counts thanks to Plating and the many +1/+1 counter synergies.

Second is Spell Snare, which seems to be out of favor these days; it goes late in most drafts, and sometimes it doesn’t get picked at all. Many of the most devastating threats to a Workshop deck are caught by a Spell Snare including Null Rod, Collector Ouphe, Hurkyl’s Recall, and Kataki, War’s Wage. Since VRD is a format largely played out across the zero to three mana value landscape, most decks will naturally have multiple Spell Snare targets, leaving it well positioned in these types of aggressive decks.

Even in a more Midrange build that leans toward powering out prison pieces or higher cost threats, Blue still offers unique tools to integrate resilience and/or consistency into a deck. An early Wurmcoil Engine is a threat. An early Wurmcoil followed by an Academy Ruins is likely a problem for a player without exile effects.

When and How

Because the Mishra’s Workshop itself is not currently in high demand, and because a large chunk of its spells are robots in which no other drafter will be interested, Aggro-Shops can be easily drafted from any position. Black Lotus is far from its most effective here, but Ancestral Recall is a boon, and fast mana is integral to building explosive opening hands. Unlike many VRD archetypes, Aggro-Shops is consistently able to make use of the extra colorless mana from a Sol Ring, enhancing its appeal even above an on-color Mox. Similarly, Mana Vault is slightly less awkward here as a colorless ritual than in some decks, and the appeal of spending as many as four of the first five picks on fast mana is strong.

Time Walk is a strong consideration here and a powerful option for any aggressive deck. Mox Opal is another priority card, and while in the past a drafter could realistically look to take both Mox Diamond and Mox Opal, the recent trend is for them to go closer together. Against sharp drafters I would expect the pattern moving forward to be both Moxen continuing to solidify as top ~26 picks, with the order of selection being determined by drafter positioning. There is more demand on Diamond because almost anyone can play it, but Opal is stronger in the decks that can play it. Additionally, Diamond is sometimes awkward in Workshop builds because they are so focused on leaning on a smaller than average number of mana sources that produce larger amounts of mana. A hand that draws just three mana sources can be just fine as long as one is an Opal/Spire/Glimmervoid, but it can be less than ideal when one of them is a Diamond needing to eat another.

Even the Workshop itself can be inconvenient at times, as it cannot be used to pay equip costs or contribute colored mana. Late in the process of goldfishing for my recent Discord draft I hit upon the notion of including a Talisman. It did some mitigating, allowing the deck to leave up countermagic protection in instances when it otherwise wouldn’t have been able to without another turn. I was very happy with how the swap of a single land for a Talisman played and will continue to deploy it with Workshops.

The card I had really wanted to spotlight with my recent draft was Urza’s Saga, an enormously versatile card that perhaps does its best work in the Modular variant where it can find the newly printed Zabaz, provide removal via Portable Hole, and grab powerful sideboard cards. The baseline was always Skullclamp though, as it would give the gas needed for a reload. In fact, I came away from that draft not thinking so much about Saga as I was about two other noticeable standouts.

First, I should have played Steelshaper’s Gift. Skullclamp and Cranial Plating were consistently the best cards I could draw. Plating is arguably the biggest threat the deck presents by virtue of turning any loose goon into a legitimate beefcake. Most of the wildest opening hands these decks produce will result from playing and equipping Plating on turn two.

In some of these instances, the creature being equipped will be the other standout, Patchwork Automaton. What I thought would be a “neat addition” turned out to be an MVP on par with the most unpleasant things you can be faced with on turn one. Ravager will always be a unique threat for the steel army, albeit one that’s better to draw later in the game, but on turn one an Automaton is actually the bigger menace.

The Bullpen

Here are some additional cards not featured in the above decklists that are sometimes deployed by drafters playing similar archetypes in the past. These cards are all playable in different builds and are worth consideration by anyone forming a deck outline for this draft plan.

VRD is a meta-game with a wide spectrum of viable decks, many of which feature a variety of modus operandi. Workshop Aggro is one of the many fun rides you can strap into, with a style of play that makes it stand out even among its fellow creature beat-down decks. So the next time you find yourself drafting a few pieces of fast mana and wondering just what to do with them, perhaps consider turning some robots sideways.
