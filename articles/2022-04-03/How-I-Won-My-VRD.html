How I Won My VRD

Written by: Dom Harvey on March 17, 2022

Rotisserie Drafts sound like they might grow old fast - a static card pool that is only marginally shaken up by most new sets and lacks the variety that the random contents of packs gives to normal booster draft. In practice, Vintage Rotisserie Draft has remained popular over the years and as soon as you try it you see why - it’s a format of endless possibilities where the public information of the drafting process creates some fascinating dynamics. I’ve enjoyed dabbling in the format wherever I can (and just won the most recent Discord draft!) so here are some thoughts on how I would approach it with the competitive but creative mindset I apply to Constructed (bearing in mind that most VRDs in practice are a more relaxed affair where not all drafters have a cutthroat focus on maximizing their win rate).

To summarize the format quickly: players take turns (this wraps around in ‘snake order’ - once the last player makes a pick in a round, they now pick again and the order reverses for this new round) to pick cards from the entire Vintage card pool. This isn’t a formally organized format so each playgroup will have their own house rules for some of the edge cases (how to treat Snow-Covered basic lands, for example). After about 45 picks, players build and battle with their 40-card decks.

With this turn order in mind, all seats are certainly not created equal. If the card pool has a few obvious power outliers (VRD has all the most powerful cards - there are enough of these that nobody walks away disappointed but also some easy picks for the first few seats) these will be snapped up by the early players in the first round. More importantly, the players at either ends of the wheel are at an advantage for the entire draft as they can make picks that are informed by each other without the risk of “wasting” a pick on a card whose value has changed by the time you get to pick again.

This is important context for my preferred approach to VRD: finding several specific combos or interactions to build around with high-quality card filtering and interaction tying the room together. You can stake your claim to one part of a combo knowing it’s not worth it for any one opponent to waste their pick to steal the other part from you unless that part has other uses - for example, if you take Painter’s Servant at any point it’s very unlikely that someone will snatch your Grindstone, but the Emrakul, the Aeons Torn you want for your Sneak Attack might be nabbed by someone hoping to pair it with Channel. Reading the room and figuring out how long you can afford to leave a desired card or combo on the table to focus on cards you know will be fought over is a key to success in this format.

The headliners of the Power 9 and guaranteed first-round picks in any VRD, these are the best cards in any deck lucky enough to have them but also change the value of other cards substantially. Adding a Mox Jet to your card pool will help your win rate no matter what; adding Black Lotus does the same but also allows you to build around the Lotus for the rest of your draft. This can mean a lower bar for high-impact but expensive cards like Jace, the Mind Sculptor, specific synergies like Lurrus/Underworld Breach/Emry, or entire strategies like Storm or artifacts matter that can dream so much bigger when they have access to Black Lotus.

Similarly, opening the draft with Ancestral Recall makes anything that copies or recasts spells much more appealing - including VRD and Constructed staples like Snapcaster Mage and Dreadhorde Arcanist. My winning deck from this VRD started with Time Walk intending to lean into it in one of several ways depending on what was open, ending up as a UR tempo deck that can convert close games to runaways quickly by finding Time Walk with Mystical Tutor/Dig Through Time and pairing it with Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy/Galvanic Iteration or Lutri as my companion.

Time Vault is a frequent first-round pick as the best combo enabler around. There are many cards that go infinite with it (well-known ones like Manifold Key/Voltaic Key or Tezzeret the Seeker, which is effectively a one-card combo with it as it searches for it and untaps it every turn, all the way through to obscure ones like Kiora’s Follower/Voltaic Servant or Mizzium Transreliquat). Many of these combos are fully colorless, allowing them to slot into basically any deck; are artifacts, making those combos even easier to assemble (and interact with); and are cheap, threatening fast wins or allowing you to spend mana finding the missing piece and still winning in the same turn.

It’s hard for a card to do more than Urza’s Saga - it’s several threats in one card that resists the most common forms of interaction and can find a combo piece, a hate card, or just grind the opponent down with even more card advantage. You can support Saga with just a single card to find with the final chapter but it scales scarily well with any incidental artifacts you have lying around. I expect Saga to become a very common pick in Rounds ~3-4 as more and more drafters see how impressive it is.

Of the familiar combo shells that people like to port to VRD, Sneak is one of the best - the counterplay is more narrow than for any of the artifact/graveyard combos, there’s some redundancy on the Sneak half (Show and Tell/Arcane Artisan, Channel, Through the Breach, Reanimator generally, Oath of Druids sometimes ), and there are so many good targets that you can wait a long time for those. The premiere targets also cross over nicely with other decks - Emrakul and Griselbrand are fine as early picks because you can find some degenerate use for them even if your intended one falls through.

A bedrock of the planeswalker control decks, Oath of Druids is backbreaking against any creature strategy and can be generously enabled via the Oko you have to pick highly or the Swan Song you can get whenever you like (as well as explicit setup cards like Forbidden Orchard). Beyond restricting you to just the creatures you want to hit, Oath asks very little of you and adds a whole new dimension to a deck that can meet that condition without much trouble. Your hits can just be good cards in your deck anyway - T3 Oath into Uro + escape Uro will put you massively ahead in any sensible game.

Looming over the early rounds of every VRD is one question: when will the run on fetchlands start? As in other Eternal formats, fetchlands are excellent fixing in their own right and have all the small synergies that warp those formats in excess like delve and library manipulation but they have a unique flexibility in the context of a rotisserie draft - off-color fixing of any other kind is useless for my UR deck but a Wooded Foothills or Flooded Strand can still do great work for me if I pick up a dual/shockland/Triome to pair with it.

This means there is naturally more competition over the fetchlands but also lessens the pressure to pick up any one in particular. My UR deck from the latest VRD couldn’t enjoy Scalding Tarn or Volcanic Island as the other URx drafter snapped those up first - but my Wooded Foothills and Steam Vents were effectively the same when I needed them to be.

Taking this to its conclusion, Prismatic Vista is the most valuable fetchland of all - it has all those added benefits while being automatically on-color without needing to use another pick on a land.

In every draft you have to strike a balance between filling out your own deck and having the right mix of maindeck/sideboard interaction to keep up with opponents trying to do their own absurd things. There’s a steep drop between Thoughtseize and any other discard spell, making it crucial for any black drafter to take it highly and some very specialized tools like Red Elemental Blast/Pyroblast don’t have good replacements. On the other hand, a green drafter won’t feel too bad about missing out on Force of Vigor even if they agree it’s the best of its kind because there’s a bottomless well of Nature’s Claim variants to turn to if you like.

This also informs the relative strengths/weaknesses of all the different proactive plans you can chase in this format. Many of the best strategies and multi-card packages revolve around artifacts but anyone can overload on artifact hate once you get to the later rounds and picking up sideboard cards is all that’s left. In turn, this makes a generally stable deck or one that has several plans that don’t share weaknesses more reliable than a deck too focused on one thing. An otherwise normal Azorius Control deck that happens to have a Time Vault or Painter-Grindstone combo presents a classic dilemma: how many cards that just tag artifacts do you even want, given that a hand with dead Naturalizes is even more likely to lose to Counterspell into Jace, the Mind Sculptor?

This highlights a flaw in what seems like an obvious plan: draft a disruptive aggro deck in an open color and have the platonic ideal of Mono-Red or Mono-White. This is a great recipe in Vintage Cube - why not try it here? The problem is that every color and strategy can pick up as many anti-aggro tools as it likes and the structure of an aggro deck (requiring a high quantity of cards that apply early pressure) makes it tough to pivot into another strategy beyond the familiar Constructed method of raising your curve to become more of a midrange deck.

My Draft

My draft from Dis7 shows off some of these principles as well as how much room there is to experiment!

P1 Time Walk - One of the most counterintuitive things about VRD is that Time Walk is generally seen as a second-tier card that doesn’t belong with the headliners of the Power 9. I suspect this is a big mistake and that in a “good Time Walk deck” having the card will massively increase your win rate when drawn - even more so if you try to build around it, which is why I knew I wanted it here. I hoped to pair it with Spellseeker/Eternal Witness/Ephemerate in a port of the Modern/Legacy deck if UGx was open or with Galvanic Iteration in UR otherwise.

P2/3 Force of Will/Force of Negation - The rare interaction that can consistently interact with fast combo starts while letting you develop your own plan at the same time.

P4 Shelldock Isle - A very early Isle to bridge the gap between some of the combo packages I was shooting for (Emrakul + cheat effects, Thassa’s Oracle) - even if I had ended up there, I could have picked this up much later instead.

P7 Urza’s Saga - I expect in a few months you won’t ever get this card this late.

P9/P2 Pyroblast/REB - Every VRD has several blue decks and those are usually the scariest competition - this draft was no exception. These would also be phenomenal against me and are the best answers to the top-shelf blue planeswalkers once they have resolved.

P10 Lutri - The closest thing to a free companion in VRD and good in context when I already plan to copy high-impact spells and want extra blue cards to pitch to my Forces.

P13 Steam Vents - Many rounds after the run on fetches/duals, I’m able to pick up Steam Vents to level up my Foothills despite there being another UR drafter.

P15 Jace, Vryn’s Prodigy - Will always play second fiddle to Snapcaster Mage in VRD but it has a special upside in my deck - recasting the spell for no additional cost makes it easier to set up the copy + Time Walk chains.

P16 Murktide Regent - As in every format with MH2 there’s now an urgent question - why would you bother with attackers in other colors when blue now has the best threat too?! Between this and the True-Name Nemesis I gladly grabbed with P22 I have a threat base that rivals any dedicated creature deck as well as the unique strengths blue gets to boast about.

This was a rather conventional deck by my usual VRD standards and was diluted by directly competing with another streamlined UR tempo deck, but the card pool here is so deep that both of us were happy with our decks in the end.
