Cast from Exile: Exploring Faldorn

Written by: Stephen Hagan on September 19, 2022

This article begins with a love of impulse draw. If you are not aware, impulse draw has become the name for the effect commonly found in red where cards are exiled from the top of your deck with the ability to be cast either the same turn, or some point in the feature. Wizards has begun what looks like a heavy lean into impulse in recent sets with cards such as Valakut Exploration, Atsushi, the Blazing Sky, Experimental Synthesizer, and VRD aggressive stand out, Laelia, the Blade Reforged. The effect has also become a bigger player in Commander with last year’s Rakdos All-Star from the Forgotten Realms set, Prosper, Tome-Bound.

Prosper himself has been a card I’ve discussed with my fellow VRD enthusiast Brandon (Dr. Peepeepoopoo MD) Curry as a potentially powerful card, with both coming to the same conclusion that is too much of a cost to make it work. More recently, in Wizards new foray into D&D, they published Faldorn, Dread Wolf Herald, a card that I instantly fell in love with. The card provides a growing board state which is built around a type of card advantage and that seems pretty powerful. My commander list for Faldorn is storm-based and pretty powerful:

VRD does not get to use such powerful effects as Jeska’s Will as well as commander does. My love affair with Faldorn and her wolves led to a pretty detailed and intense discord theory discussion asking “can this card work?” That discussion becomes this article.

Trade-Offs

Strengths:

  • Built in card advantage

  • Gains board advantage for playing impulse draw

  • Gains more advantage for Cascade and Adventure

  • Plays well with powerful cards such as Laelia, Robber of the Rich, and Ragavan.

  • In so access to fast mana

Disadvantages:

  • No haste

  • Impulse draw can backfire if you hit powerful cards early you can’t cast yet

  • Less powerful when not coming out of a command zone predictably

The last disadvantage is one that is particularly important for discussion. There are a lot of strong legendary creatures with build around abilities that do amazing things as commanders because you know you have access to them at almost any time, other than Drannith Magistrate shenanigans. Many of those might not translate over to VRD because the card is just one in 40 and you are not going to always have access to them. An example is the planeswalker Aminatou who I have great love for and does very powerful combos, but I’ve never made the jump with her because without her on demand, she is far less impressive as an engine. The potential difference for Faldorn is the fact that you are already building around card advantage and powerful effects such as Cascade and Adventure.

Theory Lists

I asked a few folks to make some theory lists after the discussion. Ones that I could share here to highlight a few ways that Faldorn or focusing on casting from exile might play out. Before I share the lists a few important notes.

This is all theoretical. You never know how something will run until you test it, these could end up 1-6 or worse the dread 0-7. I just don’t happen to think they will. Theory lists are always weird because you make assumptions about what cards you can get and what cards you cannot. Sometimes the cards just do not go your way.

Sti

Sti, our friend from across the pond, brings us a list that looks to play a tempo strategy with a solid card advantage suite.

Sti makes a fair assumption that he can get Mox Ruby and Time Walk. Both of these seem pretty straight forward and reasonable. His theory list leaves off duals/fetches. But looking at the rest of the list I think it is fair to assume we would be able to grab a few to make our mana more consistent. Getting the mana draw spells and all the key pieces might be a challenge, but I think overall this list looks reasonable. I would probably worry less about fetches and grab my duals later to make sure I got all the key pieces I wanted.

What makes this deck potentially work for me is that the aggressive pieces are all pretty strong on their own. You can win without ever drawing Faldorn, but if you do get a turn 2 Faldorn to stick you are going to put down a lot of wolves through Laelia, Shardless, Bloodbraid, and the Adventure spells. I really like the inclusion of Tlincalli Hunter, which you are pretty much playing for the re-buy Adventure half. But if you do get to the creature itself, lots of upside.

The goldfishes I’ve done of the deck felt powerful. I probably want another bounce spell for the tempo but I particularly like the Garruk Relentless as a card that can take over a game and break a stalled board. Outpost Siege is probably the one I would question the most, though if the card gets rolling it is going to be hard to lose.

DrPeepeepoopooMD

Next up we have the aforementioned DrPeepeepoopooMD. The good Doctor did not provide a finished deck list, but in a fashion true to how his brain works, a collection of the cards that he would draft and left me to build around. I didn’t. Instead we will discuss the cards as a whole and see what strategies he was leaning into.

First thing we see is that we are in , the same as Sti. I think Brazen Borrower and Shardless Agent are potentially too powerful to not include. For our first round in his theory list we see he is assuming the Ancestral Recall spot. I think if you had recall this would be a solid type of list to go into, as you are not competing over the tons of counterspells but still having good solid blue lines of play. Getting Ragavan after Ancestral is probably a stretch with how things have been going of late, but it is not unheard of. I really like the focus on DFC Lands here to make sure we are hitting our lands but making flood less impactful. He can also choose to play Food Chain combos as a possible iteration, which gives him good value out of getting cards exiled before he can cast them if he loses Griffin or Scourge. There is not a built in pay off for infinite mana from those, but you will almost always have the mana for what you need.

I lean a little more into the discard part by grabbing Currency Converter, Containment Construct, Seasoned Pyromancer, and Magmatic Channeler. Vivien and Chandra give you card advantage and flexibility. The Wrenn and Six/Strip Mine plan adds some disruption. In the end the better play might be lean more into the lands play adding Excavator/Crucible and Valakut Awakening, but I stayed away from that for this theoretical shot. I love Augur of Autumn here as it gives you some extra reach with lands and maybe even cards off the top. I also feel comfortable siding out a lot of this for stuff like Heroic Intervention or Veil of Summer. These are the types of decks that do not get hurt too bad in sideboard matches, a lot of engines have too many pieces to lose for those.

In the end, I think I like Sti’s list the best and think it has the most potential, but it also will be the hardest to get all the pieces. It is harder for me to analyze the good Doctor’s as it is more a list of cards to mold, but there are a lot of strong plays there to consider.

Closing Thoughts

I think there is a lot more room for VRD playables than some folks think. Synergy is often king. If you are thinking outside the box, look for synergy but don’t focus on building around it in such a way that you can’t win without it.

The aforementioned downside of commanders is real. A card being powerful as a commander does not always translate directly over to success in VRD.

Thought experiments are fun. Expand and push the boundaries of the format. When I first started at St. Lotus 2 the general perception was planeswalkers were ok, aggro was not overly viable, and combo/control was the top of the heap. As the meta changes so too what is viable. My earlier article about Walkers highlighted their power, but as the meta changed to more aggro and tempo, Walkers often feel like you are just behind. The format is rich and deep, and often dependent on the individual draft or the people in it. Don’t get tunnel vision.
